File 01 — Location
WHAT IS THIS PLACE

You have noclipped out of reality.

The Backrooms. Level 0. Mono-yellow wallpaper, fluorescent hum, the smell of wet carpet and something you can't name. You've seen this before — not here, but somewhere in the architecture of every bad-faith argument you've ever tried to walk through.

The TVs are not decoration. They are the debate. Every screen is a moment where a real argument was made — and ignored, deflected, or buried under noise.

The further you fall, the deeper you go. Each level down is another layer of the same conversation — the same cognitive loops, the same bad logic, echoing down through an infinite office that never ends and never changes.

The window shows you what they don't want to talk about.

File 02 — Origin
THE REAL DEBATE

Everything in this game series is real. The debates that built each stage happened — in comment sections, in livestreams, in the DMs nobody was supposed to see.

Dean Withers is a real person. His arguments are real arguments — the kind made by millions of people every day to justify the unjustifiable. He is not a villain. He is a mirror.

When someone argues against animal rights, they're not really arguing against animals. They're arguing against the part of themselves that knows.

— Earthling Official, Stage 8 Wall Text

The game series does not mock Dean Withers. It maps him. Every stage is a different corridor in the same building — the one where people get lost trying not to think about what they already know.

The backrooms are what it feels like to debate someone who will never engage. You walk the same halls. You find the same TVs. The lights flicker. There is no exit — only a deeper level.

File 04 — The Connection
THE LINK

Animal abuse and interpersonal violence share a documented relationship. Research consistently shows those who harm animals are more likely to harm people — and communities built around the normalisation of cruelty have always paid a broader social cost.

The legal system knows this too. In most countries, you can be prosecuted for torturing a dog but not a pig — despite identical neurological capacity for suffering.

The law draws a line between companion animals and farmed animals that has nothing to do with science and everything to do with money. That line is where the debate lives.
